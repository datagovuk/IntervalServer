URI have the following forms which designate calendar-aligned intevals:

id/calendar-year/{year} A specific 1 year interval.
id/calendar-half/{year}-H{half} A specific 6 month interval.
id/calendar-quarter/{year}-Q{quarter} A specific 3 month interval.
id/calendar-month/{year}-{month} A specific 1 month interval.
id/calendar-day/{year}-{month}-{day} A specific 1 day interval.
id/calendar-hour/{year}-{month}-{day}T{hour} A specific 1 hour interval.
id/calendar-minute/{year}-{month}-{day}T{hour}:{minute} A specific 1 minute interval.
id/calendar-second/{year}-{month}-{day}T{hour}:{minute}:{second} A specific 1 second interval.

Syntactic contraints on field values are intended to align with ISO 8601 syntax, with the deliberate omission of timezones (for now). Syntactic constraints are as follows (in addition requests for non-existant dates will result in 404 responses).

{year:[1-9][0-9]*} Year in as decimal number without leading zeros
{half:[12]} First or Second Half Year
{quarter:[1234]} First through forth Quarter
{month:[0-1][0-9]} Two digit month of year, starting with "01" for January
{day:[0-3][0-9]} Two digit day of month, starting with "01" as the first day of the given month.
{hour:[0-3][0-9]} Two digit hour of day, starting with "00" as the first hour of a given day.
{minute:[0-5][0-9]} Two digit minute of hour, starting with "00" as the first minute of a given hour.
{second:[0-3][0-9]} Two digit second of minute, starting with "00" as the first second of a given hour.

These URI are intended to denote particular aggregation periods for statistical data sets and it is not clear that the need to be strongly grounded in a zoned time-line. For example it would be relevant to cite a given month (or indeed month of year - but that is for later) as being a period of aggregation for statistical purposes without being absolutely grounded in a zoned timeline. That would allow statistics for the same month to be tabulated across geographies without having to be overly specific about the exact start and end time of each aggregation.
